# Wu Boshi Revealing Cases And Fixed Objects

This file explains why special cases can reveal fixed objects so effectively.

It answers:

- why do coincidence, tangency, zero slope, infinite slope, or symmetric cases reveal a fixed point or fixed line faster than the general family?

## Core Principle

The fixed object is usually not born from the special case.
It is already present in the full family.

The special case matters because:

- it strips away nonessential freedom
- it makes the hidden controller visible
- it reveals the invariant without the decorative motion

## Why Full Tracking Is Often Worse

Tracking the whole family follows visible movement.

But the target is often controlled by:

- a hidden anchor
- a conserved line
- a fixed relation
- an invariant direction

So full tracking wastes effort on parts that are not actually deciding the target.

## What A Revealing Case Does

A good revealing case:

1. preserves the decisive relation
2. removes irrelevant freedom
3. makes the hidden controller explicit

This is why tangent, coincidence, endpoint, and infinity cases are so strong:

- they are reduction devices, not lucky guesses

## Typical Revealing Cases

- tangent / coincidence
- two points merge
- slope becomes `0`
- slope becomes `∞`
- symmetric placement
- midpoint / center placement
- endpoint / vertex / boundary

## The Deeper Logic

The fixed object is often the shadow cast by the whole family.

The revealing case works because the family becomes transparent at that shadow angle.

So the route is:

- do not ask “what does every member do?”
- ask “what do all members secretly share?”

Then use the revealing case to expose that shared object.

## Hard Rule

A revealing case is valid only if the shared structure survives outside the special case.

If the special case creates a relation the general family does not inherit, it is not revealing the fixed object.
