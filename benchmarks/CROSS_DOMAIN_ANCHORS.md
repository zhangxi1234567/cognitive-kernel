# Cross-Domain Anchors

The same anti-hallucination law should survive translation.

If a cognitive rule only sounds good in one domain, it may be rhetoric rather than structure.

These anchors keep the layer honest by showing the same law in math, coding/debugging, and research/mechanism extraction.

## 1. Boundary Pressure

The fastest truth often appears at the edge.

Math:
Check endpoints, singularities, zero, infinity, symmetric placements, or collapsed dimensions.

Coding/debugging:
Check empty input, null state, first and last branch, overflow boundary, retry limit, or teardown path.

Research/mechanism extraction:
Ask where the claim should stop working, reverse, saturate, or become undefined.

Same law:
An explanation that never states its boundary is often hiding its controller.

## 2. Invariant Before Story

Keep one thing fixed and see whether the mechanism respects it.

Math:
Conservation, monotonicity, parity, sign, order, symmetry, or a quantity that should not change under recoding.

Coding/debugging:
Idempotence, count preservation, no duplicate side effects, stable sort order, checksum, reference identity, or state that must survive refactor.

Research/mechanism extraction:
A proposed mechanism should preserve what the phenomenon obviously preserves and change what it obviously changes.

Same law:
If the story explains everything but preserves nothing, it is probably too loose.

## 3. Dimension And Unit Sanity

Many false mechanisms are type errors wearing prose.

Math:
A scaling argument, dimensional mismatch, or impossible rate/area/volume relation can kill a line immediately.

Coding/debugging:
Milliseconds versus seconds, bytes versus characters, absolute versus relative paths, count versus index, sync versus async expectations.

Research/mechanism extraction:
Do not mix levels casually: token, sentence, document, user, market, population, and institution are not interchangeable units.

Same law:
A claim that crosses units without paying a translation cost is suspect.

## 4. Cheapest Counterexample

Do not reward a theory for sounding complete before it survives one hostile witness.

Math:
A chosen value, sign flip, degenerate parameter, or nearby construction that violates the claimed rule.

Coding/debugging:
The smallest repro, one log that contradicts the narrative, or one execution trace that reaches the "impossible" branch.

Research/mechanism extraction:
The closest real case the mechanism cannot explain without adding ad hoc clauses.

Same law:
One dangerous witness is often worth more than a long explanatory paragraph.

## 5. Local And Global Agreement

Many hallucinated mechanisms work only because the zoom level keeps changing.

Math:
The infinitesimal story, aggregate story, and boundary story should not contradict each other.

Coding/debugging:
Function-level reasoning should agree with system behavior; the branch story should agree with the trace.

Research/mechanism extraction:
Micro incentives, meso process, and macro outcome should connect without hidden discontinuities.

Same law:
A mechanism that needs inconsistent scales to survive is not yet a mechanism.

## 6. Smaller Truer Replacement

Verification is not only for rejection. It is also for compression.

Math:
The ornate derivation collapses to symmetry, conservation, or a one-line comparison axis.

Coding/debugging:
The suspected distributed bug becomes a single stale cache key or unit conversion error.

Research/mechanism extraction:
The social theory collapses to one constraint, one bottleneck, or one selection effect.

Same law:
Good checking does not merely say "false"; it often reveals the smaller carrier that was doing the real work.

## Anchor Test

Before trusting a benchmark claim, ask whether its governing law can be restated across these three domains without changing its shape.

If not, the rule may still be useful, but it is not yet an anchor.
